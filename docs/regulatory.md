# Regulatory Landscape — Gas Detection

## EPA Methane Rule (2024)

The EPA finalized rules mandating methane monitoring for oil and gas facilities, including existing sources (not just new construction). Key provisions:

- Continuous monitoring required at wellheads, compressor stations, processing plants
- Alternative detection technologies approved (including drones and continuous monitors)
- Applies to existing sources — massive installed base needing upgrades
- Creates demand for affordable, reliable continuous monitoring devices

**Relevance to SFG:** Continuous, multi-gas, solid-state monitoring directly addresses this mandate. Pipeline operators need devices that can run unattended for months.

## OSHA Confined Space (29 CFR 1910.146)

Before entering any confined space (tanks, vessels, below-grade areas), workers must test for:
- Oxygen deficiency/enrichment
- Combustible gases (methane, propane)
- H2S
- CO

Currently requires carrying 4 separate sensors or a multi-gas handheld with electrochemical cells that degrade.

**Relevance to SFG:** One device, solid-state, no consumable electrochemical cells.

## MSHA Mining Regulations (30 CFR Part 75)

Underground coal mines require:
- Methane warning at 1.0-1.5%
- Methane shutoff at 2.0%
- CO alert at 5 ppm, alarm at 10 ppm
- 500 consecutive successful tests required for MSHA certification
- Equipment must withstand vibration, dust, water intrusion

**Relevance to SFG:** No chemical degradation (PPLN is inert crystal). Doesn't get poisoned by silicone or H2S like catalytic bead sensors.

## EPA Fence-Line Monitoring (40 CFR 63)

Refineries must monitor benzene and other hazardous air pollutants at the facility perimeter. Current approach: open-path FTIR or passive sorbent tubes.

**Relevance to SFG:** Open-path SFG monitoring with a thermal or powered IR source. No moving parts at the detection end.
